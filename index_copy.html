Thermodynamics of Mixing

Ideal Mixing:


Assumption that substances being mixed
behave like ideal gases.

Some Equations:

$$ΔG = ΔH - TΔS$$
$$ ΔH_{mix} = 0 $$
$$ ΔS_{mix} = -R*[xlnx + (1-x)ln(1-x)] $$
$$ → ΔG_{mix} = RT*[xlnx + (1-x)ln(1-x)] $$
${x =}$ 123
${ΔG_{mix} =}$ 123
${μ_0 =}$ 123
${μ_1 =}$ 123
$$G_{total} = G + ΔG_{mix}$$


Mixing is energetically favorable. Thus a mixture of two components will have lower energy than either of the two on their own.

Use the slider bar above to change the mole fraction, X, of solute in the liquid phase (for example, adding salt to water). This will lower the Gibbs Free Energy of the liquid phase at a given temperature as seen in the top plot.

How does this affect the temperature at which the solid-liquid transition occurs in the system?

This effect is known as freezing point depression and explains why adding salt to roads in the winter causes ice to melt. It also explains why adding water to mantle rock in a subduction zone causes melting.
